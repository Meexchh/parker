01

Exploratory Data Analysis

Data Cleaning · Feature Engineering · Visualizations

Data Cleaning

756 rows with missing values were identified and handled. No duplicate rows were found. The airport and carrier columns were renamed to airport_code and carrier_code for clarity. The year variable was cast to float64.

Feature Engineering

A new on_time_flights variable was created by subtracting delayed flights (arr_del15) from total arrival flights. Two subset DataFrames were extracted: one for Delta Air Lines and one for Fort Lauderdale–Hollywood International Airport.

Distribution: Avg Delay

The distribution of average minute delays per airline–airport combo is strongly right-skewed, with a sharp peak on the left. Most pairs have low average delays, but a long tail of outlier pairs drives the mean up.

Distribution: % Delayed

The percentage of delayed flights by airline–airport combo is also right-skewed. Most pairs see fewer than 40% of their flights delayed, though some outlier pairs reach much higher proportions.

Seasonal Pattern

The grouped bar chart of average NAS delay by month shows that major airports (Detroit, Las Vegas, Los Angeles) do not consistently outpace smaller airports (Nome, San Angelo). This challenges the initial hypothesis that hub airports always suffer more NAS delays.

Weather Delay Timing

Contrary to expectations, summer months show comparably high or higher weather delays than winter months in some years. This suggests that thunderstorm season in summer may rival winter storm disruptions for flight impacts.

Statistical Inference

T-Tests · Chi-Square · Linear Regression

T-TEST HYPOTHESES

H₀ · T-Test 1

There is no difference in average minute delay due to aircraft between United Air Lines and American Airlines.

REJECT H₀ · p < 0.05
Both distributions are right-skewed with heavy outliers. Despite this, sufficient evidence exists that United and American have different average aircraft delay times.
H₀ · T-Test 2

There is no difference in average NAS delay between Ft. Lauderdale–Hollywood International and Miami International Airport.

REJECT H₀ · p < 0.05
Again, right-skewed distributions, but the p-value confirms a statistically significant difference in NAS delays between the two South Florida airports.

CHI-SQUARE HYPOTHESES

H₀ · Chi-Square 1

The frequency of delays by month is independent of airline carrier.

FAIL TO REJECT H₀ · p > 0.05
H₀ · Chi-Square 2

The severity of delays is independent of airline carrier.

REJECT H₀ · p < 0.05

Regression 1 — R² = 0.85

Number of arrival flights explains 85% of the variance in delayed flights. The coefficient is positive, confirming more flights → more delays. Year and month show negative relationships as controls.

Regression 2 — R² = 0.90

Total arrival delay minutes explain 90% of variance in NAS delay minutes. Weather and late aircraft delays show negative coefficients as controls. An excellent model fit.

Classification

Logistic Regression · L1 / L2 / Elastic Net Regularization

Outcome Variable

Binary outcome: whether an airline–airport pair has had at least one cancelled flight (1) or not (0), derived from arr_cancelled > 0.

Predictor Variables

Arrival flights, month, year, weather count, late aircraft delay, and carrier count. Selected to capture volume, timing, and reliability signals.

Baseline Model — ~0.73 F1

Logistic regression with 5-fold cross-validation achieved mean accuracy and F1 scores around 0.73 — an above-average result indicating the predictors carry real signal.

Key Coefficient: Weather Count

Weather Count was the most influential predictor. Knock-on effects from natural events (hurricanes, storms) create patterns of cancelled flights. Year was the least impactful, as annual flight volumes are relatively stable.

L1 (Lasso) Regularization

Features zeroed out: Year, Weather Count, and Carrier Count. Despite this aggressive removal, performance scores remained unchanged — confirming these features were redundant without losing explanatory power.

L2 (Ridge) & Elastic Net

Ridge shrinks all coefficients without zeroing them. Elastic Net (l1_ratio=0.5) combines both strategies. All three regularization approaches produced comparable performance, suggesting the model is robust to feature weighting strategy.

Unsupervised Learning

PCA · K-Means Clustering · Elbow Method

Dimensionality Reduction (PCA)

PCA was applied to the 6 scaled predictor features. A scree plot was used to visualize explained variance per principal component, guiding the decision to retain 3 components for downstream modeling.

Elbow Method → k=3

Inertia was calculated for k=1 through k=10. The elbow in the curve clearly appears at k=3, indicating 3 natural clusters in the airline–airport delay data.

Cluster 0 & 1

Both Cluster 0 and Cluster 1 share similar PC1 values and are tightly packed, suggesting they represent airline–airport pairs with consistent, predictable delay behavior. Cluster 1 sits slightly higher on the PC2 axis.

Cluster 2

Cluster 2 contains the majority of data points and is far more spread out across both PC1 and PC2, representing the most varied group — likely a mix of high-traffic and weather-sensitive routes.
